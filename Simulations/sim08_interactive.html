What you are looking at

A coil of N turns and area A sits in a magnetic field that oscillates in time, B(t) = B₀ cos(ωt). Only the component of the field along the coil's area vector (its normal) contributes, so the flux through one turn is Φ = A B cosθ — this carries no factor of N. Faraday's law then gives the induced emf as N times the negative rate of change of that flux, ℇ = −N dΦ/dt = N A B₀ ω sin(ωt) cosθ — the N lives with the emf. Drag the sliders and press Play; the schematic shows the coil and field, and the graph shows how flux and emf rise and fall.

Flux & induced emf vs. time

Horizontal axis = time t. Both curves are drawn at true scale on the same axes, so you can see the emf is much larger than the flux — that factor is the N turns and the rate ω. The window rescales as you drag.

What each plot shows

  • Magnetic flux Φ(t) = A·B·cosθ through one turn — no factor of N.
  • Induced emf ℇ(t) = −N dΦ/dt — includes the N turns; that is why it towers over the flux.

Questions to explore

  • Predictbefore you drag When the flux is at its maximum, will the induced emf be maximum, minimum, or zero? Play the animation and check.
  • Manipulatet, f Animate time and compare the peaks of the flux (blue) and the emf (red). How far apart in the cycle are they?
  • Explainthe red curve Why is the emf largest exactly where the flux is changing fastest, and zero where the flux is momentarily flat? (This is the meaning of ℇ = −N dΦ/dt.)
  • CalculateN, A, B₀, f For N = 10, A = 1, B₀ = 2 T, f = 0.25 Hz, work out ℇmax = N A B₀ ω, then read it off the peak-emf value beside the schematic.
  • Extendθ Set the coil tilt θ to 90°. Why do both the flux and the emf collapse to zero? Watch the area vector in the schematic.
